The Stakes: Why This Race Matters

With Gov. Henry McMaster term-limited after nearly a decade, South Carolina elects its next governor into a state undergoing real transformation — a manufacturing boom anchored by foreign auto investment, a rural healthcare crisis, the fastest-growing data center market in the Southeast, and ongoing debates about tax competitiveness, school quality, and reproductive rights.

The Republican primary is the real electoral contest: Democrats haven't won a statewide race since 2006. But the Democratic candidates represent serious policy alternatives worth understanding, and SC's demographics are slowly shifting. The GOP primary almost certainly goes to a runoff on June 23 if no candidate tops 50%.

How to read this guide: Each issue card below shows the honest case for a policy direction and the honest case against it. The comparison table shows where each candidate stands. No issue is as simple as any campaign makes it sound — this guide tries to give you both sides of every debate.

The Key Issues — Both Sides of the Debate

State Income Tax Elimination

The Case ForSC currently has one of the highest income tax rates in the Southeast (being phased from 7% to 6%). Eliminating it would make SC competitive with Florida and Tennessee, attract high-income residents and businesses, and put more money directly in workers' pockets. Neighboring states with no income tax have seen strong job and population growth.
The Case AgainstThe income tax generates billions in annual state revenue that funds schools, roads, and services. No candidate has fully detailed a replacement mechanism. Critics warn the gap could shift tax burden to sales and property taxes, which hit lower-income households harder proportionally.

SC's income tax currently accounts for roughly 40% of general fund revenue. A phase-out over many years is more realistic than an immediate cut; most candidates acknowledge this privately even if they campaign on full elimination.

AI Data Centers

The Case ForData centers bring high-paying tech-adjacent jobs, generate substantial property tax revenue, and diversify SC's economy beyond manufacturing and tourism. The tax revenue they produce could meaningfully offset the cost of income tax elimination — a direct link most candidates don't explicitly make. They also accelerate broadband and electrical infrastructure investment that benefits the whole region.
The Case AgainstHyperscale AI facilities consume enormous amounts of electricity and water. Without strict conditions, ratepayers and farmers can bear those costs. Data centers are also capital-intensive but not always labor-intensive — the job-to-investment ratio is often lower than traditional manufacturing. Some incentive deals have given companies large property tax breaks that reduce the net revenue benefit.

South Carolina voters polled strongly: they don't want utility bills to rise as a result of data center expansion. The policy question isn't whether to allow them — it's what conditions to attach.

Abortion Law

The Case For RestrictionsSupporters argue that life begins at conception or detectable heartbeat and that the state has a compelling interest in protecting unborn life. SC's current 6-week law reflects a compromise position supported by major pro-life organizations. They note it includes exceptions for rape, incest, and maternal health.
The Case Against RestrictionsCritics argue a 6-week ban — before many women know they are pregnant — is functionally a near-total ban. The proposed S.1095 would remove rape and incest exceptions and impose criminal penalties on doctors and women. Doctors warn it worsens SC's already serious maternal health and OB-GYN shortage crisis.

SC has a genuine OB-GYN shortage, especially in rural areas. Abortion restrictions and that physician shortage interact — a policy reality most campaign messaging ignores on both sides.

School Choice / Vouchers

The Case ForSchool choice gives families — especially lower-income families in failing districts — the ability to exit underperforming schools. Competition creates incentives for public schools to improve. SC's rural and lower-income communities are often most poorly served by their assigned public school, and choice programs give those parents an alternative they previously lacked.
The Case AgainstCritics argue voucher programs divert funding from public schools that serve the majority of children, leaving those schools with fewer resources. Private schools accepting vouchers are often not subject to the same accountability or accessibility standards. Early data from other states shows mixed results on academic outcomes for voucher students.

SC's existing program is relatively new. Its long-term effects on both private and public school quality in SC specifically are still being measured — honest advocates on both sides acknowledge the evidence is still developing.

Medicaid Expansion

The Case ForSC has refused federal Medicaid expansion since 2013, leaving an estimated 500,000 residents uninsured — the "coverage gap" between Medicaid eligibility and marketplace subsidies. Expansion would bring significant federal matching funds (90 cents on every state dollar), slow rural hospital closures, and cover the state's workforce in healthcare-adjacent industries. 40 states including many Republican-led states have expanded.
The Case AgainstOpponents argue Medicaid expansion grows government dependency, adds long-term state budget obligations (even with federal matching), and that the federal government may reduce its match percentage over time, leaving SC on the hook. Some argue market-based and direct primary care solutions address the same problem more efficiently.

SC has left an estimated $16–18 billion in federal funds on the table since 2013 by refusing expansion. This is one of the sharpest real-world policy differences between the parties in this race.

Roads, Bridges & Infrastructure

The Case For InvestmentSC consistently ranks among the worst states for road and bridge condition. Poor infrastructure raises costs for every SC business, reduces property values in affected communities, creates safety hazards, and makes the state less competitive for attracting new employers. Federal infrastructure funds are available; a governor who prioritizes this can leverage them aggressively.
The Reality CheckSC's road funding formula is complex and largely legislatively controlled. A governor's ability to "fix roads" is limited without legislative cooperation — and proposals to restructure road funding have repeatedly stalled. Voters should ask candidates specifically how they plan to change the funding mechanism, not just that they'll fix the problem.

SC has more state-maintained lane miles per capita than almost any other state — a legacy of a uniquely centralized system. That structure makes reform politically difficult and rarely discussed candidly on the campaign trail.

Foreign Investment & Chinese Companies

The Case For RestrictionsNational security concerns about Chinese-linked companies owning land near military installations (SC has several) or operating in critical supply chains are legitimate and bipartisan at the federal level. Restricting Chinese government-linked companies from certain sectors reflects a broader industrial policy shift shared by both Trump and Biden administrations.
The Case Against Broad BansSC's economy is built on foreign investment — BMW, Volvo, Michelin, and hundreds of supplier companies employ tens of thousands. Overly broad restrictions could conflict with trade agreements, trigger retaliation that harms existing employers, or create legal uncertainty that deters all foreign investment. The distinction between national-security threats and ordinary foreign business matters enormously in implementation.

SC has attracted over $50 billion in foreign direct investment. The challenge is surgical precision: restricting genuine national security risks without sweeping up legitimate employers that are pillars of the Upstate economy.

Predatory Lending

The Case For ReformSC has among the most permissive payday lending laws in the nation. High-interest short-term loans trap lower-income borrowers in debt cycles that undermine financial stability, reduce consumer spending in local economies, and disproportionately affect Black and rural communities. Reform advocates say rate caps and consumer protections would benefit hundreds of thousands of SC households.
The Counter ArgumentSupporters of the industry argue that short-term lenders serve customers who have no other credit access — that eliminating payday loans doesn't eliminate financial emergencies, it just removes one option. Some research suggests strict rate caps can reduce credit availability for the very populations reformers aim to help.

Democratic candidate Billy Webster co-founded Advance America, one of the nation's largest payday lenders, and now calls for reform of the industry he helped build. His credibility on this issue will face scrutiny regardless of the merits of his current position.

Judicial Selection Reform

The Case For Popular ElectionsSC is one of only two states where the legislature selects judges — creating a system where judges may feel beholden to lawmakers rather than the public. Popular elections, advocates argue, give citizens direct accountability over the courts and reduce the insider nature of judicial appointments.
The Case Against Popular ElectionsJudicial races with campaign fundraising introduce the same special-interest money problems into courts that they have in legislatures. Studies show elected judges in states like Texas and Alabama often rule in ways that favor major campaign donors. The current SC system, while unusual, insulates judges from direct campaign politics — a protection that benefits business predictability and civil rights alike.

This is an underreported issue with long-term implications for every business contract dispute, criminal appeal, and civil rights case that comes before SC courts. Both the current system and popular elections have serious, well-documented flaws.

SC-Specific Background Every Voter Should Know

SC INCOME TAX — THE MATHSC's income tax generates roughly 40% of general fund revenue. A phased elimination over 10–15 years is financially possible if growth offsets the decline; an immediate cut is not. Every candidate who campaigns on elimination is talking about a multi-decade project, not a first-term achievement.
GOVERNOR'S ACTUAL POWERSC's governor is constitutionally weaker than in most states. The legislature controls most appropriations, many agency appointments, and even some executive hiring. Promises to "fix" roads, schools, or spending often require legislative buy-in that campaign rhetoric never discusses.
MEDICAID — THE $16B QUESTIONBy refusing Medicaid expansion since 2013, SC has declined an estimated $16–18 billion in federal matching funds. The federal government pays 90 cents of every expansion dollar. Forty states, including Arkansas, Louisiana, and other GOP-led states, have accepted expansion.
SC ROAD FUNDINGSC maintains more lane miles per capita than almost any other state — a legacy of extreme centralization. The funding formula is controlled by the legislature and has resisted reform for decades. Every candidate's road promise requires legislative action they may not be able to deliver.
FOREIGN MANUFACTURING BASESC hosts BMW (Spartanburg), Volvo (Berkeley County), Michelin (Greenville), and hundreds of supplier companies. Foreign direct investment in SC exceeds $50 billion. "Restrict foreign companies" rhetoric needs careful surgical limits to avoid harming the state's largest employers.
PRIMARY RUNOFF RULEIf no Republican tops 50% on June 9, the top two candidates face a head-to-head runoff on June 23. With 5–7 serious candidates splitting the vote, a runoff is very likely. The runoff electorate is typically smaller and more conservative — this shapes how candidates are campaigning.
ACE BASINOne of the largest undeveloped estuaries on the East Coast, spanning four SC counties. Major new water consumption upstream is a legitimate ecological and agricultural concern that Kimbrell and Wilson have begun to address specifically.
S.1095 — WHAT IT ACTUALLY DOESThe Unborn Child Protection Act would ban abortion from clinical detection, remove rape/incest exceptions, classify abortion medication as Schedule IV controlled substances, impose up to 20 years/$100K on doctors, and up to 2 years on women who obtain illegal abortions. Most GOP candidates have said they'd veto it — but that position should be confirmed, not assumed.
SC OB-GYN SHORTAGESC already has a severe OB-GYN and maternal health physician shortage, particularly in rural areas. Medical associations cite abortion restrictions as a factor in physician recruitment and retention. This is a real healthcare workforce issue that intersects with the abortion debate in ways campaign messaging on both sides often ignores.
